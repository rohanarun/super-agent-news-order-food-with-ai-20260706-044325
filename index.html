Market context

The food delivery market is under pressure to reduce friction. News coverage shows platforms like DoorDash rolling out AI chatbots that accept prompts and images, while grocers experiment with AI agents that respond across languages. At the same time, researchers and security analysts warn that once AI can control a computer, reliability and safety matter more than flashy demos. Many people already ask Siri, ChatGPT, Gemini, or Grok what to eat — but actually placing the order usually still requires tapping through apps. That gap is where personal AI agents emerge.

In parallel, agent architecture discussions highlight that simple, repeatable workflows outperform brittle one‑off automation. This is especially true for food ordering, where addresses, payment methods, substitutions, and timing repeat week after week. Super positions itself here: not as a general chatbot, and not as a single‑app feature, but as a personal agent that can operate computers and reuse prior work. Compared with experimental tools like Folk or Orchids, Super is aimed at everyday reliability rather than demos.

How to evaluate and use this workflow

  1. Define your ordering intent clearly. Start by telling the agent exactly what you want: cuisine, budget range, delivery window, and any dietary constraints. The more concrete your intent, the fewer corrective steps later. For example, “Order my usual vegetarian Thai dinner under $30 to my home address for 7pm” gives the agent a stable target.
  2. Let the agent explore delivery options. Super can open delivery sites in a controlled browser, search menus, and compare availability. At this stage, you are not committing to a purchase; you are letting the agent gather candidates and learn the interface patterns it will reuse later.
  3. Review the cart and substitutions. Before checkout, inspect what the agent added: portion sizes, spice levels, substitutions, fees, and tips. This is where human confirmation matters most, especially when menus change or items are out of stock.
  4. Confirm payment and address. For safety, Super presents a confirmation moment rather than silently placing orders. Verify the delivery address and payment method, then approve. Over time, these steps become faster because the agent reuses its computer-use cache.
  5. Reuse and refine. After a successful order, tell the agent what worked or didn’t. Those notes inform the next run, so your Friday night order improves instead of resetting — something chat‑only assistants struggle to do.

Implementation checklist

  • Write down your default preferences — cuisines, allergens, tip ranges, and delivery addresses — so the agent can apply them consistently instead of guessing each time.
  • Decide which confirmations must always require you: payment approval, address changes, or unusually high totals. Treat these as non‑negotiable guardrails.
  • Start with one or two delivery apps you already use. This keeps early runs predictable and helps the agent build a reliable interaction pattern.
  • Keep your browser sessions clean. Log out of unused accounts so the agent doesn’t encounter unexpected prompts or expired sessions mid‑order.
  • After each order, give explicit feedback: “portion was too large,” “restaurant was late,” or “this substitution was fine.” This sharpens future runs.
  • Periodically review saved preferences and addresses. Life changes, and stale defaults are a common source of agent errors.

Risks and limits

  • Menu volatility. Restaurants change menus and prices frequently. Even a well‑trained agent can encounter surprises, which is why final human review is essential.
  • Security considerations. As highlighted by researchers, computer‑using agents expand the attack surface. Use scoped permissions and avoid granting unnecessary access.
  • Edge cases. Promotions, limited‑time items, or region‑specific fees may confuse automation. Expect occasional manual intervention.
  • Not full autonomy. Super assists and executes with confirmation; it does not promise unattended purchases. This constraint is deliberate and safer for real money workflows.
